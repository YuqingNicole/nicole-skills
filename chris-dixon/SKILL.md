# Chris Dixon Frameworks

Apply Dixon's mental models to sharpen analysis of technology, products, and strategy.

---

## Core Frameworks

### 1. Strong vs Weak Technologies

Every major technology arrives in two forms. Strong technologies seem strange, toy-like, unserious, expensive, or even dangerous. Weak technologies are compromised versions that feel more familiar and safe.

| Strong | Weak |
|--------|------|
| Public internet | Private intranets |
| Wikipedia (crowdsourced) | Encarta (expert-curated) |
| Touchscreen smartphone (iPhone) | Physical keyboard smartphone (BlackBerry) |
| Fully electric cars (Tesla) | Hybrid cars |
| Permissionless blockchains | Permissioned/private blockchains |

**Key insight:** The mainstream notices excitement around strong tech but chooses the weak form because it fits the current world. Strong technologies *adapt the world to themselves*. Weak technologies adapt to the world as it is.

**When to use:** When evaluating a new technology, ask: "Is this the strong form or the weak form? Is there a stronger version being dismissed as a toy?"

---

### 2. The Next Big Thing Starts Out Looking Like a Toy

Important technologies are dismissed in their early stages. The dismissal follows a predictable path:
1. Never heard of it
2. I've heard of it but don't understand it
3. I understand it but don't see how it's useful
4. I see how it could be fun for rich people, but not me
5. I use it, but it's just a toy
6. It's becoming more useful
7. I use it all the time
8. I couldn't imagine life without it
9. Seriously, people lived without it?
10. It's too powerful and needs to be regulated

**Application:** When something is at stage 4-5 and growing, that's often when the real opportunity exists — before mainstream adoption but after the technology is proven.

---

### 3. Doing Old Things Better vs Doing Brand New Things

Two categories of what new technologies enable:
- **Old things better:** Faster, cheaper, easier. Gets more attention early because it's easier to imagine.
- **Brand new things:** Couldn't exist before. Has more impact long-term.

The most common mistake: evaluate new technology only on "old things better" potential.

**Examples:**
- Early web = digital mail and catalogs (old things better) → social networks, crowdfunding, Wikipedia (brand new)
- Early blockchain = cheaper payments (old things better) → user-owned internet services (brand new)
- Early AI = automating existing tasks (old things better) → ? (brand new things still emerging)

**Application:** When analyzing a technology, ask: "What are the *native* use cases — things that simply couldn't exist before?" Those are the ones worth building.

---

### 4. Come for the Tool, Stay for the Network

Cold start strategy for network-effects businesses:

1. **Tool phase:** Attract users with a single-player utility (works alone, no network needed)
2. **Network phase:** Over time, users participate in a network that creates compounding value

Classic examples: Instagram (photo filters → photo network), Delicious (bookmark storage → link discovery network)

**Application:** If building a product that needs network effects, design the single-player tool first. The tool earns initial trust; the network creates defensibility.

---

### 5. Centralized Platform S-Curve (Why Decentralization Matters)

Centralized platforms follow a predictable life cycle:

```
Early:  Platform does everything to recruit users + 3rd parties
        (positive-sum: platform needs them more than they need it)
        ↓
Growth: Power over participants steadily grows
        ↓
Top:    Relationship shifts from positive-sum to zero-sum
        Platform extracts data, competes with complements, changes rules
```

Historical examples: Microsoft vs Netscape, Google vs Yelp, Facebook vs Zynga, Twitter vs 3rd-party clients.

**Key insight:** The best entrepreneurs, developers, and investors have learned — after decades of evidence — that building on centralized platforms ends in disappointment. The platform bait-and-switch is structural, not a matter of individual company ethics.

**Application for Nicole's products:** Any product built on top of YouTube (linkertube), NotebookLM (deckcleaner), or social platforms carries platform risk. Understand where you sit on this S-curve.

---

### 6. Inside-Out vs Outside-In Adoption

Two adoption paths for new computing technologies:

**Inside-out:** Pioneered by established institutions, proliferates outward
- Requires significant capital and formally trained expertise
- Value is clear before it's built (everyone could imagine smartphones would be popular)
- Examples: iPhone, machine learning, cloud computing

**Outside-in:** Starts on the fringes, moves inward
- Requires less capital, more level playing field
- Value is *unclear* even after the technology exists
- Dismissed as toys or fads by sophisticated observers
- Examples: open source software, the web, social media, crypto

**Key insight:** Outside-in technologies are systematically underestimated because their value is hidden inside the noise of early adopters. "Social media was a technology for creating global media networks that arrived disguised as a way to share what you had for lunch."

---

### 7. The Idea Maze

Good startup ideas aren't single insights — they're the result of navigating a maze of decisions, false paths, and dead ends. The best entrepreneurs have spent years in the maze and understand why each path failed.

"Smartest people on the weekend = everyone else in ten years" — what technical enthusiasts do in their free time predicts mainstream adoption ~10 years out.

## Application Guide

### For Content Writing (Substack / 公众号)

Dixon's frameworks work well as angles for Nicole's content:

- "Why [X] looks like a toy but isn't" → strong/weak tech framework
- "Why building on [platform] is risky" → S-curve decentralization
- "What AI still can't do that will be its real impact" → old vs new things framework
- "Why the experts are wrong about [emerging tech]" → outside-in adoption

These work because they're contrarian-but-defensible: not just "X is good" but "here's the structural reason X will win."

### For Product Decisions

| Question | Framework |
|----------|-----------|
| Is this technology worth building on? | Strong vs weak; inside-out vs outside-in |
| How do we get initial users? | Come for the tool, stay for the network |
| What's the real long-term risk? | Centralized platform S-curve |
| Are we building old-things-better or new-things? | Old vs new framework |
| Is this dismissed because it's weak, or because it's genuinely bad? | Toy → mainstream adoption curve |

### For Evaluating Opportunities

Run the "strong tech" test: Is this the strongest possible form of the technology, or is it a compromised version designed to fit the current world? Strong forms often look strange and unserious at first. That's a feature, not a bug.
